Risks — coverage gaps & fragile markets

Trends — what stands out by geography & service

Weight Loss is the engine — and the most stretched.
Success by Sesame is the single largest line at 31% of all bookings (4,988), and it's consistently short of providers across states. Demand for GLP-1/weight-loss care is outrunning supply faster than anywhere else, which is why the top of every recruiting list is a weight-loss segment.
Two supply regimes by service line.
High-throughput, async-friendly care runs hot — Fertility (18.7 bookings/provider), Everyday Rx (17.6), and Weight Loss (16.7) are thin on providers everywhere. Relational, recurring care is the opposite: Mental Health holds the most providers of any line (324, ~29% of all supply) against only moderate demand, making it the one line near balance — and the only place we see real slack.
The Sun Belt is where demand has outrun supply.
California looks comparatively healthy (1.2× load on a deep bench of 153 providers), while the fast-growing Sun Belt — TX, FL, GA, AZ, NC — runs 1.65×. Supply has clustered in established coastal markets and hasn't kept pace with newer, high-growth demand in the South.
Fertility is a structural specialist-scarcity problem.
It carries the highest load per provider (18.7) on the thinnest bench in the marketplace: 32 of 51 states have one or zero fertility providers, and five states have live bookings with none at all. It's the hardest line to recruit for and the easiest to leave uncovered.
Demand is concentrated; coverage gaps sit in the long tail.
The top 10 states drive 54% of bookings, so raw shortfall follows population (the Southeast carries the largest absolute gap). But the 19 "no-coverage" segments — live demand with zero providers — cluster in small states and low-volume women's-health lines (Women's Health, Fertility, PCOS). That's a small-market access problem, not a volume one.
Low-volume women's-health lines are fragile, not just small.
Women's Health and PCOS run on one or two providers per state, so the absolute gaps look minor — but coverage is one departure away from zero in dozens of markets. Worth watching even though they never top a volume-ranked list.

Caveat: this is one month of synthetic data, and figures reflect the currently selected Load Index target (defaults to a flat 10), which inflates apparent under-supply. Treat magnitudes as directional and the patterns as the takeaway.

Methods, More Ideas & Caveats

Method

Two ways to set the target (toggle at top). The flat target holds every service line to the same bar — pick 10, 15, or 18 bookings per provider per month. It's simple, but it judges a fertility visit and a weight-loss refill at the same throughput, so the count of under-supplied segments depends heavily on where you set the bar (~525 providers short at 10, far fewer at 18). The separate per-service-line method instead scores each line against its own typical throughput (median bookings per provider: Everyday Rx 16.5, Mental Health 13, Weight Loss 15, Women's Health 11, Fertility 16.25, PCOS 9), which normalizes across services and puts the total shortfall around ~141. TX Weight Loss leads under every setting, so the headline is robust; the flat views are simpler screens and the service-line view the more like-for-like one.

On the service-line target. Using each line's median is transparent but imperfect: if the whole marketplace is under-supplied, the median is itself somewhat stretched, so it can understate true capacity. A stronger version would anchor to the throughput of well-supplied markets, or better, to provider utilization if we had it.

More ideas — where I'd take this next

Track how long patients wait to be seen. The load index is a floor, not the true gap, because bookings are censored (see caveats). The single highest-value new input would be time-to-first-available-appointment or abandoned-search data — it's censoring-aware and it's what patients actually experience. I'd anchor the system on that and keep the ratio as a secondary check.

Focus on markets where demand is growing fastest. One month can't separate a growing gap from noise. Month-over-month growth per segment would prioritize where demand is rising — a segment at 1.3× growing 30%/mo is more urgent than a static 1.8×.

Rank by how easy each gap is to fill. Weigh the gap against effort-to-fill, not gap alone — a weight-loss NP is fast to recruit and credential; a fertility specialist is slow and scarce. This would reorder the recruiting list toward best ROI on PS effort.

Move idle providers into under-supplied states. The over-supplied markets are an asset, not just waste: a clinician sitting idle in a slack state (e.g. CA mental health, ~7 bookings per provider) can be cross-listed to serve an under-supplied one. The licensing compacts make this cheap — physicians via the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact and RNs via the Nurse Licensure Compact can practice across member states on one credential, turning existing capacity into new coverage with zero net-new hires. It also cuts the retention risk from starved providers. One caveat: compacts port the license, but scope and prescriptive authority are still set state-by-state, so this is cleaner for physicians than for NPs in restricted-practice states.

Add more nurse practitioners and PAs. Protocol-driven lines — weight loss, everyday Rx — are well suited to nurse practitioners and physician assistants, who are faster and cheaper to add than physicians. Expanding NP/PA supply in each under-supplied state is often the quickest way to close a gap, provided the supervising-physician structure is in place where states require it (e.g. Texas).

Prioritize by revenue, not just booking volume. A gap in weight-loss/GLP-1 or fertility is worth more per fill than Everyday Rx, so a "dollars-at-risk" priority would follow value, not just volume (needs real cash prices to wire in).

Consider a better core metric to manage to. The load index proxies two things it doesn't measure directly — unmet demand and provider retention. A sharper pair would be time-to-appointment (demand side) plus provider utilization (supply side). Two corollaries: "over-supplied" should mean below a viability floor, not just below the median (some slack is healthy — it gives patients choice and buffers churn); and because more supply improves availability and converts more bookings, "recruit to target" probably undershoots.

Caveats

Read the gap as a floor, not a ceiling. Bookings are realized demand. In under-supplied segments some patients never booked because no one was available, so true demand — and the real gap — is likely larger than shown. Segments with 0 providers but live bookings are flagged "No coverage."

Assumptions & data limits. Every active provider is treated as equally available (no utilization or hours data, so headcount may overstate real capacity); bookings are a proxy for demand and aren't unique patients (async refills may inflate weight-loss volume); one month is treated as steady state (no seasonality or trend); and this is synthetic data built for the exercise. Not in the dataset but valuable next: utilization, wait times, price/revenue, churn/ratings, and multi-state licensing.
